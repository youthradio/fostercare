A Journey Through Foster Care

Growing Up In Foster Care
Foster care is supposed to be temporary, but that’s not always the case. Youth Radio takes you through the experience of young people who grow up -- and age out -- in foster care.

Entering Foster Care
It all starts with a court-order : a social worker or judge determines it is not in your best interest to remain with your biological parents, and you are removed from their home. But you’re only a kid -- you still need a place to live and someone to care for you. You enter the foster care system, a state-run program that’s supposed to be a temporary placeholder until you can move to a more permanent, safe and caring home.




Where Do You Live?

As you enter foster care, the state determines your case plan, which includes a goal for where you will end up permanently. Most of the time (55%) this goal is to reunify with your parents. Other times, the goal is adoption (25%) or other forms of permanent placement. In the meantime, you will live with adults who are certified as foster parents or caretakers.

You just turned 18 while still in the foster care system. Now what?



Depending on where you live, you may or may not have “aged out” of foster care. Based on the needs of older foster youth, many states have extended the amount of time before you lose benefits associated with your foster status.

Older Foster Youth (18-21)


In 2008, a federal law allowed states to opt to raise the age cutoff for foster care from 18 to as old as 21. So far, only 21 states have decided to raise the age limit to 21. If you live in one of these states, you are entitled to certain resources relating to needs like housing, health care, and education. For example in California, older foster youth can receive a monthly housing stipend, access a court appointed special advocate, qualify for health care, and get priority enrollment in state universities and community colleges.
